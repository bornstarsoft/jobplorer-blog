Some job postings may mention AI-assisted art, animation, design, programming, production, testing, localization, or content workflows. ## What AI-assisted Game Development Roles Can Mean

AI-assisted game development can refer to many different role types and workflows. Depending on the employer and project, it may involve:

- Technical art or pipeline work.
- Concept exploration or visual development support.
- Tooling for artists, designers, writers, or programmers.
- Quality assurance, testing, or production support.
- Data, analytics, or player-support workflows.
- Internal documentation, localization, or content operations.

These examples are starting points, not universal role definitions. Employers may use different titles for similar work, and similar titles can involve different tools or responsibilities.

## Where Game Developers Can Research Opportunities

Game developer job seekers can compare several sources:

- Studio career pages for official openings and application instructions.
- General job sites for broad role discovery.
- Professional networking platforms for company and role research.
- Game industry job boards or communities, when relevant.
- Publisher, platform, or tooling company career pages.
- Portfolio, art, code, or project communities that may help with role research.

No single channel guarantees better results. A practical approach is to compare multiple sources and keep notes on role titles, requested skills, portfolio expectations, and application instructions.

## How to Read Job Postings Carefully

When a posting mentions AI, machine learning, generative tools, automation, tools engineering, or content pipelines, read the full posting carefully. Useful questions include:

- Is the role focused on art, design, programming, production, QA, tooling, data, or another area?
- Are AI tools required, preferred, or only mentioned as part of the workflow?
- Does the employer list specific portfolio, demo reel, code sample, or work sample expectations?
- Are tool requirements clearly stated by the employer?
- Does the posting explain whether the role is full-time, contract, remote, hybrid, or on-site?
- Are application steps, deadlines, and document requirements clear?
- Does the employer provide an official career page or applicant help page?

If a job-site summary and the employer's own page differ, use the official employer posting as the main source.

## How to Prepare Portfolio and Application Materials Responsibly

Game development applications often depend on role-specific materials. A portfolio, resume, demo reel, code sample, project page, or case study should accurately reflect the applicant's own work and contribution.

When AI-assisted work is included, review employer instructions carefully. Some employers may ask applicants to explain process, authorship, tools used, or team contribution. Others may have different rules for submitted materials. Requirements can vary by studio, role, country, platform, and project.

Before applying, check whether the employer or platform provides rules about portfolio content, AI-generated assets, work samples, tests, or assessments.

## What Not to Assume About AI-related Game Jobs

Do not assume that:

- Every game studio uses AI tools in the same way.
- A specific AI tool is required unless the posting says so.
- AI experience replaces core role skills or portfolio review.
- A tool certification guarantees eligibility for a role.
- A job-site recommendation means the applicant meets all requirements.
- AI-assisted portfolio work will be accepted without explanation.
- A role title means the same responsibilities at every studio.

AI-related workflows can be part of game production, but job seekers should still verify official requirements and use careful judgment.

## Practical Checklist for Game Developer Job Seekers

Before applying to an AI-related game development role:

- Read the official job posting from start to finish.
- Check the studio or employer career page.
- Identify whether AI tools are required, preferred, or only mentioned.
- Review portfolio, demo reel, code sample, or work sample instructions.
- Keep your resume and portfolio accurate.
- Be clear about your own contribution to projects.
- Save a copy of the posting and application instructions.
- Review job-site policies if applying through a platform.
- Check employer guidance on submitted materials, tests, and assessments.
- Avoid adding tool claims or experience claims you cannot support.

This checklist can help organize research, but it does not ensure a specific hiring result.
